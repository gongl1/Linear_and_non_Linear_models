The constant (or intercept) represents the expected value of the dependent variable when all the independent variables are equal to zero. Without an intercept, the regression model would be forced to pass through the origin (0,0), which often does not make sense in practical scenarios. Including an intercept allows the model to account for the average outcome when all predictors are absent, providing a more accurate and interpretable model.

z-value

The z-value (or z-score) is a measure of how many standard deviations an element is from the mean. In the context of regression analysis, the z-value for a coefficient estimates the number of standard deviations the coefficient is away from 0. It is calculated as the ratio of the coefficient to its standard error (

z= standard error / coefficient

A high absolute value of the z-score (either positive or negative) indicates that the observed coefficient is far from 0, suggesting a stronger evidence against the null hypothesis (which typically states that the coefficient is equal to zero, implying no effect).

P>|z| (p-value)

The P>|z| value, or p-value, indicates the probability of observing the data (or something more extreme) assuming that the null hypothesis is true. In simpler terms, it helps determine the statistical significance of each coefficient.

A small p-value (typically 0.05) suggests that there is strong evidence against the null hypothesis, allowing us to reject the null hypothesis and conclude that the coefficient is significantly different from 0. This implies that the predictor is likely to have a real effect on the dependent variable.

A large p-value suggests that we do not have enough evidence to reject the null hypothesis, and the coefficient may not be significantly different from 0, implying that the predictor may not have a significant effect on the dependent variable.
